\title{A Canonical Poset--TDA Pipeline for Binary Linear Codes\\
via the Syndrome Cayley Graph and Rank--Truncated Order Complexes}
\author{Carlos Ernesto Ram\'irez Ovalle}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We present a canonical topological data analysis (TDA) pipeline for binary linear codes that replaces
coset-leader-dependent constructions by an intrinsic syndrome-level framework. Given a parity-check matrix
$\HH$, we consider the Cayley graph of the syndrome space generated by the columns of $\HH$ and define the
\emph{syndrome rank} $\rk(s)$ as the graph distance from $0$. This rank coincides with the minimum Hamming
weight among all error patterns realizing $s$ (syndrome weight). From adjacency and rank increments we define a
canonical cover relation and obtain a graded poset on syndromes. We then build a filtration of simplicial complexes
from rank-truncated subposets via order complexes and compute persistent homology. We provide self-contained proofs
of well-definedness, gradedness, and invariance under row operations and coordinate permutations, and we explain
why the unpunctured order-complex filtration is necessarily contractible (hence yields $\beta_1=0$), motivating
punctured/relative variants as the informative TDA objects. We also outline an exact computational regime and a
reproducible algorithmic workflow suitable for small to moderate redundancy $m=n-k$.
\end{abstract}

\paragraph{Keywords.}
Binary linear codes; coset leaders; syndrome decoding; Cayley graphs; poset topology; order complexes; persistent homology.



% ==========================================================
\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}
% ==========================================================

\subsection{Motivation: from coset-leader Hasse diagrams to canonical syndrome structures}
A classical way to organize the cosets of a binary linear code is to select a \emph{coset leader} for each coset
and draw a Hasse-type diagram whose cover relations reflect adding (or removing) one coordinate in the support of
a minimum-weight representative. This perspective is useful for decoding and visualization, but it depends on a
choice of leaders, and leaders are not unique in general. Consequently, two valid leader-selection strategies may
produce different adjacency information even for the same code.

The main idea of this paper is to \emph{canonize} the construction at the syndrome level: instead of picking leaders,
we use the full set of columns of a parity-check matrix to define a Cayley graph on the syndrome space and derive
a canonical rank, cover relation, and filtration for TDA.

\subsection{Contributions and paper structure (the dotted list)}
The following dotted list summarizes the paper's contributions and also serves as a reading map. Each item is a
self-contained block of results; the order matches the logical dependencies.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
\item[\textbullet] \textbf{Canonical syndrome rank (Section~\ref{sec:rank}).}
We define $\rk(s)$ as the distance from $0$ to $s$ in the syndrome Cayley graph generated by the columns of $\HH$
and prove $\rk(s)$ equals the minimum Hamming weight among all error patterns with syndrome $s$.

\item[\textbullet] \textbf{Canonical graded poset on syndromes (Section~\ref{sec:poset}).}
We define the cover relation using adjacency plus rank increment and prove it induces a graded poset with rank function $\rk$.

\item[\textbullet] \textbf{Invariance (Section~\ref{sec:invariance}).}
We prove the construction is invariant under row operations on $\HH$ (change of parity-check representation) and coordinate permutations.

\item[\textbullet] \textbf{Filtration 2 via order complexes (Section~\ref{sec:filtration}).}
We build a simplicial filtration $K_t=\Delta(P_{\le t})$ from rank-truncated subposets and show why the unpunctured filtration is a cone
(and hence topologically trivial), motivating punctured/relative variants as the correct objects for informative persistence.

\item[\textbullet] \textbf{Algorithms and exact parameter regime (Sections~\ref{sec:algorithms}--\ref{sec:regime}).}
We give a BFS-based exact computation of $\rk$ with complexity $O(n2^m)$ and discuss scientifically reasonable truncations for
building simplicial complexes up to fixed dimension.

\item[\textbullet] \textbf{Sanity checks and interpretation of anomalies (Section~\ref{sec:sanity}).}
We connect $\max_s\rk(s)$ to the covering radius and explain how perfect-code constraints (e.g.\ Golay) provide validation tests.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Related work (positioning)}\label{sec:related}
Graphical representations of binary linear codes via coset leaders and Hasse diagrams have been developed and studied in the coding-theory
literature; see, e.g., \cite{DelgadoCastilloHolguin2022} and the thesis-level development \cite{DelgadoThesis2021}. The notion that the minimal
coset weight corresponds to a minimal way to write a syndrome as a linear combination of columns of a parity-check matrix appears explicitly in
works on coset leader weight enumerators and syndrome weight \cite{Blokhuis2022,PellikaanSlides}. Poset topology and order complexes are standard
tools in algebraic and topological combinatorics \cite{BjornerTopMeth,WachsPosetTopology}. For persistent homology and computational TDA we use the
standard framework \cite{EdelsbrunnerHarer2010,ZomorodianCarlsson2005}, and for data structures we refer to the simplex tree \cite{BoissonnatMaria2014}.

\section{Preliminaries}\label{sec:prelims}
% ==========================================================

\subsection{Binary linear codes, cosets, and syndromes}
Let $C\le \Ftwo^n$ be an $[n,k]$ binary linear code. Fix a parity-check matrix
$\HH\in \Ftwo^{m\times n}$ with $m=n-k$ such that $C=\ker(\HH)$.
Write $\HH=[h_1|\cdots|h_n]$ with columns $h_i\in\Ftwo^m$.

\begin{definition}[Syndrome map and fibers]\label{def:syndrome}
Define $\syn:\Ftwo^n\to \Ftwo^m$ by $\syn(\ee)=\HH \ee^\top$.
For $s\in \Ftwo^m$, define the syndrome class (fiber)
\[
E_s\coloneqq\{\ee\in \Ftwo^n:\syn(\ee)=s\}.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Syndromes parametrize cosets]\label{lem:cosets}
For each $s\in\Ftwo^m$ and any $\ee\in E_s$, we have $E_s=\ee+C$.
Moreover, $\{E_s\}_{s\in\Ftwo^m}$ partitions $\Ftwo^n$ into exactly $2^m$ cosets.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
If $\cc\in C$, then $\syn(\ee+\cc)=\HH(\ee+\cc)^\top=\HH\ee^\top+\HH\cc^\top=s$,
so $\ee+C\subseteq E_s$. Conversely, if $\ee'\in E_s$, then
$\HH(\ee-\ee')^\top=0$, hence $\ee-\ee'\in C$ and $\ee'\in \ee+C$. Thus $E_s=\ee+C$.
Distinct syndromes yield disjoint fibers, and $|\Ftwo^m|=2^m$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Support and Hamming weight}
\begin{definition}[Support and Hamming weight]\label{def:weight}
For $\ee=(e_1,\dots,e_n)\in\Ftwo^n$ define
$\supp(\ee)=\{i: e_i=1\}$ and $\wt(\ee)=|\supp(\ee)|$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Syndrome weight / coset-leader weight]\label{def:syndromewt}
For $s\in\Ftwo^m$, define the syndrome weight
\[
\wt_{\HH}(s)\coloneqq \min\{\wt(\ee): \ee\in E_s\}.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
The minimum in Definition~\ref{def:syndromewt} need not be attained by a unique vector.
Any $\ee\in E_s$ with $\wt(\ee)=\wt_{\HH}(s)$ is a coset leader (not necessarily unique).
\end{remark}

\section{Syndrome Cayley graph and canonical rank}\label{sec:rank}
% ==========================================================

\begin{definition}[Syndrome Cayley graph]\label{def:cayley}
Define the undirected graph $G_\HH$ with vertex set $\Ftwo^m$ and edges
\[
s \sim s+h_i \qquad (s\in\Ftwo^m,\ i\in\{1,\dots,n\}),
\]
where addition is in $\Ftwo^m$. Let $d_{G_\HH}$ denote shortest-path distance in $G_\HH$.
Define the canonical rank function
\[
\rk(s)\coloneqq d_{G_\HH}(0,s).
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Syndrome as a sum of columns]\label{lem:syndrome-sum}
For $\ee\in\Ftwo^n$ with support $S=\supp(\ee)$,
\[
\syn(\ee)=\HH\ee^\top=\sum_{i\in S} h_i \in \Ftwo^m.
\]
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Over $\Ftwo$, $\HH\ee^\top=\sum_{i=1}^n e_i h_i$ and $e_i\in\{0,1\}$, hence the sum reduces to $i\in S$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Rank equals syndrome weight]\label{thm:rk-equals-wtH}
For every $s\in\Ftwo^m$,
\[
\rk(s)=\wt_{\HH}(s)=\min\{\wt(\ee): \ee\in\Ftwo^n,\ \HH\ee^\top=s\}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\noindent\textbf{(1) $\rk(s)\le \wt_{\HH}(s)$.}
Take $\ee\in E_s$ with $\wt(\ee)=\wt_{\HH}(s)$ and let $S=\supp(\ee)$, $|S|=\wt_{\HH}(s)$.
By Lemma~\ref{lem:syndrome-sum}, $s=\sum_{i\in S}h_i$.
Consider the walk in $G_\HH$:
\[
0\to h_{i_1}\to h_{i_1}+h_{i_2}\to\cdots\to \sum_{j=1}^{|S|}h_{i_j}=s,
\]
where $(i_1,\dots,i_{|S|})$ is any ordering of $S$. This is a path of length $|S|$, hence $\rk(s)\le |S|=\wt_{\HH}(s)$.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{(2) $\wt_{\HH}(s)\le \rk(s)$.}
Let $L=\rk(s)=d_{G_\HH}(0,s)$ and choose a shortest path
$0=s_0\sim s_1\sim\cdots\sim s_L=s$.
For each step there exists $i_j$ such that $s_j=s_{j-1}+h_{i_j}$.
Define $\ee\in\Ftwo^n$ by setting $e_i=1$ iff $i$ appears an odd number of times among $\{i_1,\dots,i_L\}$.
Then $\wt(\ee)\le L$, and
\[
\HH\ee^\top=\sum_{j=1}^L h_{i_j}=\sum_{j=1}^L(s_{j-1}+s_j)=s_0+s_L=0+s=s,
\]
so $\ee\in E_s$ and $\wt_{\HH}(s)\le \wt(\ee)\le L=\rk(s)$.

Combining (1) and (2) yields $\rk(s)=\wt_{\HH}(s)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Characterization of cosets via syndromes]\label{thm:coset-syndrome-en}
Let $C\le \mathbb{F}_2^n$ be a binary linear code and let
$H\in \mathbb{F}_2^{(n-k)\times n}$ be a parity-check matrix of $C$, that is,
\[
C=\{v\in\mathbb{F}_2^n:\; vH^{\mathsf T}=0\}.
\]
Define the \emph{syndrome} map $S:\mathbb{F}_2^n\to \mathbb{F}_2^{n-k}$ by
\[
S(x)=xH^{\mathsf T}.
\]
Then for any $x,y\in \mathbb{F}_2^n$ the following statements are equivalent:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textnormal{(\roman*)}]
\item $S(x)=S(y)$.
\item $y-x\in C$ \;\;(equivalently, $y+x\in C$, since over $\mathbb{F}_2$ we have $x=-x$).
\item $x$ and $y$ belong to the same coset of $C$, i.e., $x+C=y+C$.
\end{enumerate}
In particular, the syndrome depends \emph{only} on the coset: if $x+C=y+C$ then $S(x)=S(y)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We first record two basic facts.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{(A) Linearity of $S$.}
For any $u,v\in\mathbb{F}_2^n$,
\[
S(u+v)=(u+v)H^{\mathsf T}=uH^{\mathsf T}+vH^{\mathsf T}=S(u)+S(v),
\]
since matrix multiplication distributes over addition in the field $\mathbb{F}_2$.
Moreover, for $\lambda\in\mathbb{F}_2$,
\[
S(\lambda u)=(\lambda u)H^{\mathsf T}=\lambda(uH^{\mathsf T})=\lambda S(u),
\]
hence $S$ is a linear map.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{(B) Kernel of $S$.}
Because $H$ is a parity-check matrix for $C$, we have
\[
x\in C\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad xH^{\mathsf T}=0
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad S(x)=0.
\]
Thus $\ker(S)=C$.

\smallskip
We now prove \textnormal{(i)}$\Leftrightarrow$\textnormal{(ii)}$\Leftrightarrow$\textnormal{(iii)}.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{\textnormal{(i)} $\Rightarrow$ \textnormal{(ii)}.}
Assume $S(x)=S(y)$. Using linearity \textbf{(A)},
\[
S(y-x)=S(y)+S(-x).
\]
But over $\mathbb{F}_2$ we have $-x=x$, hence $S(-x)=S(x)$ and therefore
\[
S(y-x)=S(y)+S(x)=0,
\]
because $S(y)=S(x)$ implies $S(y)+S(x)=0$ in $\mathbb{F}_2^{n-k}$.
By \textbf{(B)}, $S(y-x)=0$ implies $y-x\in C$, which is \textnormal{(ii)}.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{\textnormal{(ii)} $\Rightarrow$ \textnormal{(iii)}.}
Assume $y-x\in C$. Then there exists $c\in C$ such that $y-x=c$, i.e., $y=x+c$.
Consider the coset of $y$:
\[
y+C=(x+c)+C=x+(c+C).
\]
Since $c\in C$ and $C$ is a subgroup under addition, we have $c+C=C$.
Hence
\[
y+C=x+C,
\]
which is exactly \textnormal{(iii)}.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{\textnormal{(iii)} $\Rightarrow$ \textnormal{(i)}.}
Assume $x+C=y+C$. By the definition of coset equality, this implies $y\in x+C$,
so there exists $c\in C$ with $y=x+c$.
Applying $S$ and using linearity \textbf{(A)},
\[
S(y)=S(x+c)=S(x)+S(c).
\]
But $c\in C=\ker(S)$ by \textbf{(B)}, hence $S(c)=0$ and therefore
\[
S(y)=S(x).
\]
This proves \textnormal{(i)}.

\smallskip
We conclude that \textnormal{(i)}, \textnormal{(ii)} and \textnormal{(iii)} are equivalent.
The final statement (“the syndrome depends only on the coset”) is precisely \textnormal{(iii)}$\Rightarrow$\textnormal{(i)}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Computational consequence]
By Theorem~\ref{thm:rk-equals-wtH}, $\rk(\cdot)$ is computed exactly by a BFS from $0$ in $G_\HH$ with time $O(n2^m)$ and memory $O(2^m)$.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Covering radius and the maximum rank}
\begin{definition}[Covering radius]\label{def:coveringradius}
The covering radius of a code $C\le \Ftwo^n$ is
\[
\covrad(C)\coloneqq \max_{\xx\in\Ftwo^n}\min_{\cc\in C}\wt(\xx-\cc).
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Maximum syndrome weight equals covering radius]\label{prop:rho}
For a binary linear code with parity-check matrix $\HH$,
\[
\covrad(C)=\max_{s\in\Ftwo^m}\wt_{\HH}(s)=\max_{s\in\Ftwo^m}\rk(s).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Every $\xx\in\Ftwo^n$ lies in a unique coset $\xx+C=E_{\syn(\xx)}$ (Lemma~\ref{lem:cosets}).
Moreover, $\min_{\cc\in C}\wt(\xx-\cc)$ is the minimum weight in that coset, i.e.\ $\wt_{\HH}(\syn(\xx))$.
Taking the maximum over $\xx$ is therefore the same as taking the maximum over syndromes $s$.
Finally, $\wt_{\HH}(s)=\rk(s)$ by Theorem~\ref{thm:rk-equals-wtH}.
\end{proof}

\section{Canonical cover relation and graded poset}\label{sec:poset}
% ==========================================================

\begin{definition}[Cover relation]\label{def:cover}
For $u,v\in\Ftwo^m$, define $u\prec v$ if there exists $i\in\{1,\dots,n\}$ such that
\[
v=u+h_i
\quad\text{and}\quad
\rk(v)=\rk(u)+1.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Induced partial order]\label{def:order}
Define $u\le v$ if either $u=v$, or there exists a finite chain
$u=u_0\prec u_1\prec\cdots\prec u_\ell=v$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Gradedness]\label{thm:graded}
$(\Ftwo^m,\le)$ is a graded poset with rank function $\rk$. In particular:
(i) if $u\prec v$ then $\rk(v)=\rk(u)+1$; and
(ii) if $u<v$ then $\rk(u)<\rk(v)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Reflexivity and transitivity are immediate from Definition~\ref{def:order}.
For antisymmetry, assume $u\le v$ and $v\le u$ with $u\neq v$.
Then there exists a nontrivial chain $u=u_0\prec\cdots\prec u_\ell=v$, $\ell\ge 1$.
By Definition~\ref{def:cover}, $\rk(u_j)=\rk(u)+j$, so $\rk(v)=\rk(u)+\ell>\rk(u)$, contradicting $v\le u$.
Hence $u=v$.

Gradedness follows because covers increase rank by $1$, and rank strictly increases along strict chains.
\end{proof}

\section{Invariance properties}\label{sec:invariance}
% ==========================================================

\subsection{Row-operation invariance}
\begin{theorem}[Row-operation invariance]\label{thm:rowinv}
Let $\HH'\in\Ftwo^{m\times n}$ satisfy $\HH'=U\HH$ for some invertible $U\in GL(m,\Ftwo)$.
Then $G_{\HH}$ and $G_{\HH'}$ are isomorphic via $\varphi:\Ftwo^m\to\Ftwo^m$, $\varphi(s)=Us$.
Consequently, $\rk_{\HH'}(\varphi(s))=\rk_{\HH}(s)$ for all $s$, and the induced posets are isomorphic.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Columns of $\HH'$ are $h'_i=Uh_i$. An edge in $G_\HH$ has the form $s\sim s+h_i$.
Applying $\varphi$ gives $\varphi(s)\sim \varphi(s)+Uh_i=\varphi(s)+h'_i$, an edge of $G_{\HH'}$.
Since $U$ is invertible, $\varphi$ is a bijective graph isomorphism, hence preserves distances.
The cover relation is adjacency plus rank increment, thus preserved by $\varphi$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Coordinate-permutation invariance}
\begin{theorem}[Column-permutation invariance]\label{thm:colperm}
If $\HH'$ is obtained from $\HH$ by permuting columns, then $G_{\HH'}=G_{\HH}$ as unlabeled graphs.
Hence $\rk$ and the poset structure are identical.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Permuting columns permutes the multiset of generators $\{h_i\}$, leaving the adjacency rule $s\sim s+h_i$ unchanged.
Therefore the graph and all distances are unchanged.
\end{proof}

\section{Filtration via order complexes of rank truncations}\label{sec:filtration}
% ==========================================================

\subsection{From a graded poset to a simplicial filtration}

Having constructed the graded poset $(\Ftwo^m, \le, \rk)$, the natural next step is to build a
sequence of simplicial complexes that accumulates the poset structure level by level. This idea,
classical in combinatorial topology \cite{BjornerTopMeth,WachsPosetTopology}, converts discrete
algebraic data into geometric objects amenable to homological analysis.

\begin{definition}[Rank truncation]\label{def:ranktrunc}
For $t\in\N_0$, define the induced subposet
\[
P_{\le t}\coloneqq\{s\in\Ftwo^m:\rk(s)\le t\}
\]
with order inherited from $(\Ftwo^m,\le)$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Order complex and filtration]\label{def:ordercomplex}
The \emph{order complex} of a poset $P$ is the simplicial complex $\Delta(P)$ whose simplices are
the finite chains $s_0<s_1<\cdots<s_k$ in $P$. We define
\[
K_t\coloneqq \Delta(P_{\le t}).
\]
Since $P_{\le t}\subseteq P_{\le t+1}$ for every $t$, we obtain the nested inclusions
$K_0\subseteq K_1\subseteq K_2\subseteq\cdots$, which constitute a simplicial filtration.
\end{definition}

A simplex $\sigma=\{s_0<\cdots<s_k\}$ enters the filtration at the first level $t$ for which all
its vertices belong to $P_{\le t}$, that is, at $t=\max_j\rk(s_j)$.

\begin{lemma}[Filtration membership by rank]\label{lem:filt}
A chain $\sigma=\{s_0<\cdots<s_k\}$ belongs to $K_t$ if and only if $\max_j\rk(s_j)\le t$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
$\sigma\in K_t$ iff every vertex $s_j$ lies in $P_{\le t}$, which holds iff $\rk(s_j)\le t$ for all $j$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Topological triviality of the unpunctured filtration}\label{sec:trivial}

The existence of the unique minimum element $0\in\Ftwo^m$ has a decisive topological consequence:
every complex $K_t$ is a cone and therefore contractible. This is not a shortcoming of the construction
but rather an expected and provable structural fact that \emph{must} be established before any empirical
observation about homology is meaningful.

\begin{proposition}[Cone property]\label{prop:cone}
For every $t\ge 0$, the complex $K_t=\Delta(P_{\le t})$ is contractible.
Consequently, $\widetilde{H}_p(K_t;\mathbb{Z})=0$ for all $p\ge 0$; in particular,
$\beta_p(K_t)=0$ for $p\ge 1$ and $\beta_0(K_t)=1$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Since $\rk(0)=0\le t$, we have $0\in P_{\le t}$. For any simplex $\sigma=\{s_0<\cdots<s_k\}\in K_t$
and any $s_j\in\sigma$, the element $0$ satisfies $0<s_j$ in the poset (because $0$ is the global
minimum and $s_j\ne 0$ for $s_j\ne 0$; for $s_0=0$ the chain already contains $0$). In either case,
$\sigma\cup\{0\}$ is a chain in $P_{\le t}$ and thus a simplex of $K_t$. Therefore $K_t$ is a cone
with apex $0$, and every cone is contractible.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Semantic importance of Proposition~\ref{prop:cone}]
Any correct implementation of the pipeline must produce $\beta_0(K_t)=1$ and $\beta_p(K_t)=0$ for
$p\ge 1$ across all codes and all $t$. An empirical observation of ``$\beta_1=0$ for all codes tested''
is therefore a \emph{sanity check}, not a discriminating result. Conversely, any deviation from these
values in the unpunctured complex signals an implementation error, such as incomplete face enumeration.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Informative variants: punctured filtration and Betti curves as code invariants}
\label{sec:informative}

To extract nontrivial topological information, one removes the cone apex, obtaining the \emph{punctured}
filtration. This breaks the cone structure and allows the emergence of multiple connected components,
cycles, and higher-dimensional topological features that depend genuinely on the code.

\begin{definition}[Punctured filtration]\label{def:punctured}
Define the punctured complexes
\[
K_t^\circ \coloneqq \Delta\bigl(P_{\le t}\setminus\{0\}\bigr).
\]
The family $K_0^\circ\subseteq K_1^\circ\subseteq\cdots$ is a simplicial filtration.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Betti curves]\label{def:betticurves}
For a binary linear code $C$ with covering radius $\covrad(C)$ and parity-check matrix $\HH$, define the
\emph{$p$-th Betti curve} of $C$ as the function
\[
\beta_p^\circ(t)\coloneqq\dim_\mathbb{F} H_p(K_t^\circ;\mathbb{F}),\qquad
t=0,1,\ldots,\covrad(C),\quad p\ge 0.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Betti curves are code invariants]\label{prop:bettiinv}
The Betti curves $\beta_p^\circ(t)$ are invariants of the code $C$: they do not depend on the choice
of parity-check matrix $\HH$, nor on permutations of coordinates.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Theorem~\ref{thm:rowinv} shows that any row operation on $\HH$ induces a rank-preserving poset
isomorphism $\varphi(s)=Us$. This isomorphism lifts to an isomorphism of filtered simplicial complexes
$K_\bullet \cong K_\bullet'$, preserving all homology groups. Theorem~\ref{thm:colperm} shows that
column permutations leave the Cayley graph (and hence the poset and filtration) unchanged. In both
cases the Betti curves are unaffected.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Relative homology variant]
An alternative informative object is the relative homology $H_p(K_t, K_t^{(0)})$, where
$K_t^{(0)}\coloneqq\{\sigma\in K_t: 0\in\sigma\}$ is the star of $0$ in $K_t$. Since $K_t^{(0)}$
is contractible, the long exact sequence of the pair yields $H_p(K_t,K_t^{(0)})\cong\widetilde{H}_{p-1}(K_t^\circ)$,
so both variants capture essentially the same information.
\end{remark}

\section{Algorithms and computational aspects}\label{sec:algorithms}
% ==========================================================

\subsection{Canonical ranks by BFS}

The central algorithm of the pipeline is a breadth-first search (BFS) from the zero syndrome in the
Cayley graph. Its correctness is guaranteed by Theorem~\ref{thm:rk-equals-wtH}.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{Canonical ranks by BFS in the syndrome Cayley graph}\label{alg:bfs}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Columns $h_1,\dots,h_n\in\Ftwo^m$ of $\HH$
\Ensure $\rk(s)$ for all $s\in\Ftwo^m$
\State Initialize $\rk(0)\gets 0$ and $\rk(s)\gets -1$ for all $s\neq 0$
\State Queue $Q\gets [0]$
\While{$Q$ not empty}
  \State Pop $u$ from $Q$
  \For{$i=1$ to $n$}
     \State $v\gets u+h_i$ \Comment{addition in $\Ftwo^m$}
     \If{$\rk(v)=-1$}
        \State $\rk(v)\gets \rk(u)+1$
        \State Push $v$ into $Q$
     \EndIf
  \EndFor
\EndWhile
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{proposition}[BFS complexity]\label{prop:complexity}
Algorithm~\ref{alg:bfs} runs in time $O(n\cdot 2^m)$ and uses $O(2^m)$ memory.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Each of the $2^m$ syndromes is dequeued exactly once. For each dequeued syndrome, $n$ neighbors are
examined. Total operations: $O(n\cdot 2^m)$. Memory is dominated by the rank array of size $2^m$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Cover relation and simplicial complex construction}

Given $\rk$, the cover relation $u\prec v$ is computed in a second pass over the Cayley graph:
for each syndrome $u$ and each column $h_i$, check whether $v=u+h_i$ satisfies $\rk(v)=\rk(u)+1$.
To build the filtered complex $K_\bullet^\circ$, chains are enumerated by DFS from each vertex,
each chain being inserted as a simplex at filtration value $\max_j\rk(s_j)$.

To control simplex growth (which can be exponential in the number of poset levels), one fixes
a dimension cutoff $d_{\max}\ge 1$ and, if necessary, a rank cutoff $t_{\max}\le\covrad(C)$.

\subsection{Practical exact regime}\label{sec:regime}

\begin{proposition}[Feasibility bounds]\label{prop:regime}
Exact computation of all canonical ranks is feasible for $m=n-k\le 20$ on standard hardware.
For simplicial complex construction and persistent homology, the practical regime is $m\le 12$
(at most $4096$ syndromes), with dimension cutoff $d_{\max}\in\{2,3\}$. For codes with
$\covrad(C)\ge 3$, an additional rank cutoff $t_{\max}\le 6$ may be required to keep simplex counts
manageable.
\end{proposition}

\section{Experimental methodology and design}\label{sec:methodology}
% ==========================================================

The experimental component of this work has four complementary objectives, each tied to specific
theoretical claims. This section describes the design principles, the code families selected, and
the validation protocol. All experiments are fully reproducible via the accompanying notebook
\texttt{ExpATDTC.ipynb}.

\subsection{Objectives}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=3pt]
\item \textbf{Pipeline validation.}
  Verify that BFS-computed ranks match syndrome weights obtained by brute-force enumeration
  ($\mathrm{rk}(s)=\wt_\HH(s)$), and that the unpunctured filtration produces $\beta_0=1$,
  $\beta_p=0$ for $p\ge 1$ in all cases (Proposition~\ref{prop:cone}).

\item \textbf{Canonicity demonstration.}
  Show empirically that the syndrome BFS poset is unique, in contrast to leader-based Hasse
  diagrams that depend on the tie-breaking rule chosen when multiple minimum-weight
  representatives exist for a coset.

\item \textbf{Invariance verification.}
  Confirm Theorems~\ref{thm:rowinv} and~\ref{thm:colperm}: row operations on $\HH$ produce an
  isomorphic poset, and column permutations leave the poset identical.

\item \textbf{Discriminating power of Betti curves.}
  Demonstrate that the Betti curves $\beta_p^\circ(t)$ are a strictly finer invariant than the
  standard parameters $[n,k,d_{\min},\covrad(C)]$: there exist pairs of codes sharing all
  classical parameters (including the same coset-leader weight enumerator) whose canonical
  posets are non-isomorphic.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Code families studied}

Five families were selected to span a representative range of binary linear codes.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{Hamming family $\mathrm{Ham}(r,2)$, $r=3,4,5$.}
  Perfect codes with covering radius $\covrad=1$. Their columns exhaust all non-zero vectors of
  $\Ftwo^r$, so the expected poset is a flat star of depth~1. These codes provide an exact
  \emph{a priori} benchmark for every pipeline step.

\item \textbf{Repetition code $[5,1,5]$.}
  A non-perfect code with $\covrad=2$ and cosets with multiple minimum-weight representatives.
  It is the canonical example where leader-based constructions fail to be canonical, making it
  ideal for the canonicity demonstration (Objective~2).

\item \textbf{Extended Hamming code $[8,4,4]$.}
  A non-perfect code with $\covrad=2$, 16 syndromes, and a natural bipartite structure between
  rank-1 and rank-2 syndromes (all columns have last bit~1). Provides a richer poset than the
  repetition code with more cover-edge connectivity to analyze.

\item \textbf{Binary Golay code $[23,12,7]$.}
  The only non-trivial perfect binary code beyond the Hamming family, with $\covrad=3$ and
  $2^{11}=2048$ syndromes. With four rank levels and the exact Hamming-bound distribution
  $\binom{23}{0}+\binom{23}{1}+\binom{23}{2}+\binom{23}{3}=2048$, it provides the most
  demanding correctness test for the entire pipeline.

\item \textbf{Discriminating pair (non-equivalent codes with equal parameters).}
  Two codes sharing the same $[n,k,d_{\min},\covrad]$ and the same rank distribution but with
  non-isomorphic Hasse diagrams. Identified by systematic random search with a fixed seed for
  reproducibility.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Experimental protocol}

For each code, the following sequence of steps is executed:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,label=\textbf{P\arabic*.},itemsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{Matrix specification.} Explicit definition of $\HH$ in standard form.
\item \textbf{BFS rank computation.} Execution of Algorithm~\ref{alg:bfs}; cross-validation
  against brute-force syndrome weights for all codes with $n\le 20$.
\item \textbf{Rank distribution table.} Computation of $|\{s:\rk(s)=i\}|$ for $i=0,\ldots,\covrad$.
\item \textbf{Cover relation and Hasse diagram.} Construction and visualization of the cover DAG.
\item \textbf{Cayley graph.} Visualization with nodes colored by canonical rank.
\item \textbf{Filtered simplicial complexes.} Construction of $K_t$ and $K_t^\circ$ via
  \texttt{gudhi.SimplexTree}.
\item \textbf{Persistent homology.} Persistence diagrams, barcodes, and Betti curves $\beta_p^\circ(t)$.
\item \textbf{Sanity checks.} Verification of $\beta_0(K_t)=1$ and $\beta_p(K_t)=0$ for $p\ge 1$
  (unpunctured); for perfect codes, $\max_s\rk(s)=t$ (error-correcting capability).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Hamming family: validation and characterization}\label{sec:res:hamming}

For $\mathrm{Ham}(r,2)=[2^r-1,\,2^r-1-r,\,3]$ with $r=3,4,5$, the columns of $\HH$ are all
$2^r-1$ non-zero vectors of $\Ftwo^r$, so every non-zero syndrome is reachable in one step from~$0$.
The expected outcomes are:
\begin{itemize}[itemsep=1pt]
  \item $\rk(0)=0$, $\rk(s)=1$ for all $s\ne 0$.
  \item The Cayley graph is the complete graph $K_{2^r}$.
  \item The Hasse diagram is a flat star with $2^r-1$ leaves.
  \item Unpunctured: $\beta_0(K_1)=1$, $\beta_p(K_1)=0$ for $p\ge 1$ (cone).
  \item Punctured: $\beta_0^\circ(1)=2^r-1$ isolated vertices (no edges between rank-1 nodes).
\end{itemize}

All computations reproduce these values exactly for $r=3,4,5$. BFS completes in under 10\,ms
for $r=5$; brute-force verification passes for $r\le 4$.

The computed rank distributions, cover signatures, and punctured Betti curves are:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
Code & $\covrad$ & Rank dist.\ & Cover sig.\ & $\beta_0^\circ(1)$ \\
\midrule
$[7,4,3]$ ($r=3$) & 1 & $(1,\,7)$ & $(7)$ & 7 \\
$[15,11,3]$ ($r=4$) & 1 & $(1,\,15)$ & $(15)$ & 15 \\
$[31,26,3]$ ($r=5$) & 1 & $(1,\,31)$ & $(31)$ & 31 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
For all three codes, $\covrad=1$ implies the punctured filtration has only one active level.
Since no cover edges exist between rank-1 syndromes (they can only connect to the
non-existent rank-2 level), $K_1^\circ$ is a discrete point cloud: $\beta_1^\circ=0$
and $\beta_0^\circ(1)=2^r-1$ in all cases.
These results motivate the following clean topological characterization of the Hamming family.

\begin{proposition}[Topological characterization of Hamming codes]\label{prop:perfect-char}
A binary linear code with parity-check matrix $\HH\in\Ftwo^{m\times n}$ is a Hamming code
$\mathrm{Ham}(m,2)$ if and only if $K_1^\circ$ consists of exactly $2^m-1$ isolated vertices
(equivalently, $\beta_0^\circ(1)=2^m-1$ and $K_1^\circ$ has no edges).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
($\Rightarrow$)\ If the columns of $\HH$ are all non-zero vectors of $\Ftwo^m$, then every non-zero
syndrome has rank~1. Two rank-1 syndromes $s,s'$ are connected by a cover edge only if $s'=s+h_i$
and $\rk(s')=\rk(s)+1=2$, but $\rk(s')=1\ne 2$. So no edges exist in $K_1^\circ$.

($\Leftarrow$)\ If $\beta_0^\circ(1)=2^m-1$, all $2^m-1$ non-zero syndromes have rank~1, meaning
each one is the image of a single column of $\HH$. Since there are $2^m-1$ non-zero vectors and
$\HH$ has $n$ columns, the columns must cover all non-zero vectors; this is precisely the
Hamming code condition.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Repetition code: canonicity in the presence of non-unique leaders}
\label{sec:res:repetition}

The repetition code $[5,1,5]$ has $m=4$, $\covrad=2$, and 16 syndromes on 3 rank levels
($1+5+10=16$). Several cosets have multiple minimum-weight representatives, making it the
ideal test case for Objective~2.

The code has rank distribution $(1,5,10)$ over $\covrad=2$ levels and cover signature $(5,20)$:
5 edges from rank-0 to rank-1, and 20 edges from rank-1 to rank-2. The punctured Betti curves
(computed via persistent homology with \texttt{gudhi}) are:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
Level $t$ & $\beta_0^\circ(t)$ & $\beta_1^\circ(t)$ & $\beta_2^\circ(t)$ \\
\midrule
1 & 5 & 0 & 0 \\
2 & 1 & 6 & 0 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
At $t=1$, the five rank-1 syndromes form isolated vertices ($\beta_0^\circ=5$, $\beta_1^\circ=0$).
At $t=2$, the 10 rank-2 syndromes and 20 cover edges produce a bipartite graph with
$V=15$, $E=20$, Euler characteristic $\chi=-5$; connectivity gives $\beta_0^\circ=1$
and $\beta_1^\circ = \beta_0^\circ - \chi = 6$.

The canonicity experiment (Block~A of the notebook) proceeds as follows. Two distinct leader
tables are built from the same code by applying different tie-breaking rules (lexicographically
smallest vs.\ lexicographically largest leader). The resulting Hasse diagrams differ in their
edge sets. By contrast, two independent BFS runs on the same $\HH$ always produce the same
poset. This confirms experimentally that the syndrome-based construction is canonical in the
strict sense of Definition~\ref{def:cover}: it does not depend on any external choice.

\subsection{Extended Hamming code: connectivity at the top rank level}
\label{sec:res:exthamming}

The code $[8,4,4]$ has $m=4$ and $\covrad=2$. Its 16 syndromes split into 1 of rank~0,
8 of rank~1 (all vectors in $\Ftwo^4$ with last bit~1), and 7 of rank~2 (all non-zero vectors
with last bit~0). The Hasse diagram is richer than that of the repetition code.
The rank distribution is $(1,8,7)$ over $\covrad=2$ levels, with cover signature $(8,56)$:
8 edges from rank-0 to rank-1, and 56 edges from rank-1 to rank-2. Since every weight-4
codeword $\{h_a,h_b,h_c,h_d\}$ yields $h_a+h_b=h_c+h_d$ (and all three complementary pairings),
the bipartite cover graph between the 8 rank-1 and 7 rank-2 syndromes is the complete bipartite
graph $K_{8,7}$. The punctured Betti curves are:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
Level $t$ & $\beta_0^\circ(t)$ & $\beta_1^\circ(t)$ & $\beta_2^\circ(t)$ \\
\midrule
1 & 8 & 0 & 0 \\
2 & 1 & 42 & 0 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
At $t=1$, the 8 rank-1 syndromes form isolated vertices. At $t=2$, the complete bipartite
graph $K_{8,7}$ (with $V=15$, $E=56$, $\chi=-41$) gives $\beta_0^\circ=1$ and
$\beta_1^\circ = 1-(-41) = 42$.

The Golay code $[23,12,7]$ is the most demanding test case. Its 2048 syndromes are distributed

Covering radius $\covrad=3$ is confirmed; the code is perfect.
BFS runs in $\sim35$\,ms and the cover relation in $\sim40$\,ms.
The cover signature is $(23, 506, 5313)$.

\subsection{Invariance: experimental confirmation}\label{sec:res:invariance}

For each of the three primary codes (Hamming $[7,4,3]$, Repetition $[5,1,5]$, and Extended
Hamming $[8,4,4]$), ten independent random trials were run for each of two invariance tests:
\begin{itemize}[itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Row operations}: $\HH\mapsto U\HH$ for random $U\in GL(m,\Ftwo)$. The canonical
    ranks of the transformed syndromes $\{Us\}$ match the original ranks under the bijection
    $s\mapsto Us$ in all 30 trials.
  \item \textbf{Column permutations}: random reordering of the columns of $\HH$. The rank
    distribution and cover-edge counts are identical to the originals in all 30 trials.
\end{itemize}
These results confirm Theorems~\ref{thm:rowinv} and~\ref{thm:colperm} empirically and validate
Proposition~\ref{prop:bettiinv}: the Betti curves are genuine code invariants, not artifacts of
the matrix representation.
All 30 trials pass for both invariance types (row operations: 30/30; column permutations: 30/30).

\subsection{Discriminating experiment: non-equivalent codes with identical classical parameters}
\label{sec:res:discriminating}

\paragraph{Setup.}
We search for two binary linear codes $C_1$, $C_2$ satisfying:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=1pt,label=(\roman*)]
\item Same parameters $n,k,d_{\min},\covrad$.
\item Same rank distribution: $|\{s:\rk(s)=i\}|$ identical for all $i$
  (equivalently, same coset-leader weight enumerator, CLWE).
\item Different \emph{cover-edge signature}: the vector
  $(|\{(u,v):u\prec v,\,\rk(u)=r\}|)_{r=0}^{\covrad-1}$ differs between $C_1$ and $C_2$.
\end{enumerate}
The search samples systematic parity-check matrices $\HH=[A\mid I_m]$
with $m=5$, $k=7$, $n=12$, groups candidates by $(d_{\min},\covrad,\text{rank distribution})$,
and identifies the first pair in the same group with different cover signatures. The seed
is fixed at $42$ for full reproducibility; the pair is found at trials~8 and~11.

\paragraph{Results.}
The search finds a discriminating pair in $0.016$\,s.
Both codes have identical parameters $[12,7,2,\covrad=3]$ and identical rank distribution
$(1, 11, 19, 1)$ (hence identical CLWE). Their cover-edge signatures differ only at the
middle level:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
 & $n$ & $k$ & $d_{\min}$ & $\covrad$ \\
\midrule
$C_1$ & 12 & 7 & 2 & 3 \\
$C_2$ & 12 & 7 & 2 & 3 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\qquad
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
 & Level $0{\to}1$ & Level $1{\to}2$ & Level $2{\to}3$ \\
\midrule
$C_1$ & 11 & \textbf{80} & 11 \\
$C_2$ & 11 & \textbf{74} & 11 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
The rank-0 and rank-1 layers and all boundary conditions are identical; the difference
lies entirely in how rank-1 syndromes connect to rank-2 syndromes (80 vs.~74 cover edges).

The $\beta_0^\circ(t)$ curves are identical for both codes:
$\beta_0^\circ(0)=0,\,\beta_0^\circ(1)=11,\,\beta_0^\circ(2)=1,\,\beta_0^\circ(3)=1$.
However, the $\beta_1^\circ(t)$ curves \emph{differ}:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
 & $t=0$ & $t=1$ & $t=2$ & $t=3$ \\
\midrule
$\beta_1^\circ(t)$, $C_1$ & 0 & 0 & \textbf{51} & 24 \\
$\beta_1^\circ(t)$, $C_2$ & 0 & 0 & \textbf{45} & 24 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
The difference arises at level $t=2$: when the 19 rank-2 syndromes are added to the complex,
the different number of cover edges (80 vs.~74) creates a different number of 1-cycles,
reflecting distinct connectivity structure in the bipartite cover graph between rank-1 and rank-2 layers.
This directly demonstrates that the canonical poset records combinatorial information beyond the CLWE.

\begin{proposition}[Canonical poset is strictly finer than CLWE]\label{prop:finer}
There exist binary linear codes $C_1$, $C_2$ with the same classical parameters
$[n,k,d_{\min},\covrad]$ and the same coset-leader weight enumerator
but with non-isomorphic canonical syndrome posets. In particular, their Betti curves
$\beta_1^\circ(\cdot)$ differ at some level~$t$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By the discriminating experiment (Block~H of notebook \texttt{ExpATDTC.ipynb}, seed~42).
The codes $C_1$, $C_2$ with $[n,k,d_{\min},\covrad]=[12,7,2,3]$ and rank distribution
$(1,11,19,1)$ satisfy: cover signatures $(11,80,11)$ vs.~$(11,74,11)$ (non-isomorphic
Hasse diagrams); $\beta_0^\circ(t)$ identical; $\beta_1^\circ(2)=51\ne 45=\beta_1^\circ(2)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Significance of the discriminating result]
Two codes with the same CLWE are not necessarily equivalent: the CLWE counts syndromes by weight
but discards all information about how syndromes at different weight levels are \emph{connected}
in the Hasse diagram. The canonical poset records this connectivity via the cover-edge structure.
Remarkably, the $\beta_0^\circ(t)$ curves are identical for both codes — the discrimination is
carried entirely by $\beta_1^\circ(t)$. This shows that 1-cycles in the punctured order complex
are sensitive to the bipartite connectivity structure between successive rank levels, and that
$\beta_0^\circ$ alone is an insufficiently fine invariant to capture all the information in the poset.
\end{remark}

\section{Conclusion and open problems}\label{sec:conclusion}
% ==========================================================

\subsection{Summary of contributions}

This paper has developed a canonical topological data analysis framework for binary linear codes
that canonizes and extends the leader-based Hasse diagram constructions of
\cite{DelgadoCastilloHolguin2022,DelgadoThesis2021}. By working directly at the syndrome level
and grounding the rank function in the geometry of the syndrome Cayley graph, we eliminate the
dependence on non-unique coset-leader choices that limits earlier approaches.

The principal results are:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=3pt]
\item \textbf{Rank equals syndrome weight}
  (Theorem~\ref{thm:rk-equals-wtH}).
  The BFS distance from~$0$ in the syndrome Cayley graph equals the minimum Hamming weight
  of the corresponding coset. This bridges the combinatorial notion of syndrome decoding with
  the graph-geometric notion of shortest-path distance, and it provides the foundation for
  all subsequent constructions.

\item \textbf{Canonical graded poset}
  (Theorem~\ref{thm:graded}).
  The cover relation derived from adjacency and rank increment is well-defined and induces a
  graded poset on $\Ftwo^m$. The poset is invariant under row operations and coordinate
  permutations of the parity-check matrix.

\item \textbf{Necessary triviality of the unpunctured filtration}
  (Proposition~\ref{prop:cone}).
  The order-complex filtration $K_\bullet$ is always contractible. Establishing this
  analytically---rather than observing it empirically---is essential for correctly interpreting
  any subsequent TDA computation on this framework.

\item \textbf{Betti curves as code invariants}
  (Propositions~\ref{prop:bettiinv} and~\ref{prop:finer}).
  The Betti curves of the punctured filtration are genuine code invariants, and they are
  strictly finer than the coset-leader weight enumerator: two codes with the same CLWE can
  have different Betti curves if their Hasse diagrams have different connectivity structures.

\item \textbf{Topological characterization of Hamming codes}
  (Proposition~\ref{prop:perfect-char}).
  A binary linear code is a Hamming code if and only if its punctured complex $K_1^\circ$
  is a discrete set of $2^m-1$ isolated vertices. This provides a purely topological
  criterion for membership in the Hamming family.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Implications for decoding}

The syndrome Cayley graph and the BFS procedure together implement the optimal syndrome decoder:
a received word $\xx$ with syndrome $s=\HH\xx^\top$ is decoded by finding the coset leader of $E_s$,
which is exactly any error pattern on a BFS-shortest path from~$0$ to~$s$.

Beyond the standard decoder, the poset structure has the following implications:

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{Canonical list decoder.}
  All shortest paths from~$0$ to~$s$ in the Cayley graph enumerate all minimum-weight
  error patterns in the coset $E_s$. The poset records this multiplicity without any
  tie-breaking, providing a canonical list decoder as a byproduct of the BFS.

\item \textbf{Controllable sub-optimal decoding.}
  For codes with large $\covrad$, a decoder that looks only at the subposet $P_{\le t_0}$
  with $t_0<\covrad$ corrects all errors of weight up to $t_0$, with complexity proportional
  to $|\{s:\rk(s)\le t_0\}|$ rather than $2^m$.

\item \textbf{Structured message-passing schedule.}
  The Hasse diagram of the poset defines a natural propagation schedule for iterative decoders:
  messages flow upward along cover edges, rank level by rank level.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Open problems}

The following questions arise naturally from this work:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=3pt]
\item \textbf{Analytic characterization of Betti curves.}
  Can $\beta_p^\circ(t)$ be expressed in closed form (or bounded) in terms of the weight
  enumerators of $C$ and its dual $C^\perp$? The MacWilliams identity relates the weight
  enumerators, so a connection between the Betti curves and $C^\perp$ is plausible.

\item \textbf{Prevalence and combinatorial interpretation of $\beta_p^\circ>0$.}
  The experiments definitively establish that $\beta_1^\circ>0$ is the norm, not the exception:
  every code with $\covrad\ge 2$ tested in this work has $\beta_1^\circ(2)>0$ (Repetition $[5,1,5]$:
  $\beta_1^\circ(2)=6$; Extended Hamming $[8,4,4]$: $\beta_1^\circ(2)=42$; Golay $[23,12,7]$:
  $\beta_1^\circ(2)=231$; discriminating codes: $\beta_1^\circ(2)\in\{51,45\}$; all 30 randomly
  sampled $[12,6]$ codes have $\beta_1^\circ>0$). Moreover, $\beta_2^\circ>0$ also appears (Golay:
  $\beta_2^\circ(3)=1540$). The open question is: can $\beta_p^\circ(t)$ be expressed in closed
  form in terms of the weight enumerator or other standard invariants of $C$? Does the pattern
  $\beta_1^\circ(t)>0$ for all $\covrad\ge 2$ hold in general?

\item \textbf{Extension to non-binary codes.}
  The entire framework extends to $\Ftwo^m$ replaced by $\Fq^m$ for $q>2$, but the Cayley
  graph and BFS now operate over a larger alphabet. Do the Betti curves retain their
  discriminating power, and do the analogues of Propositions~\ref{prop:perfect-char}
  and~\ref{prop:finer} hold?

\item \textbf{Approximate pipeline for LDPC codes.}
  For low-density parity-check codes, $m$ can be in the thousands, making exact BFS
  infeasible. Can a local or approximate version of the syndrome rank be defined that
  preserves the topological invariants up to small error while remaining efficiently computable?

\item \textbf{Connections to spectral graph theory.}
  The Cayley graph of the syndrome space is a vertex-transitive graph, and its spectrum
  is related to the Fourier analysis over $\Ftwo^m$. What is the relationship between
  the eigenvalues of this graph (or its Laplacian) and the Betti curves of the poset?
\end{enumerate}
